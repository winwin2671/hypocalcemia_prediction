Abstract 1 
(240 words — within the 250-word limit) 2 
Introduction: Postoperative hypocalcemia is a common complication of endoscopic thyroidectomy and may affect 3 
postoperative recovery. Previous prediction models have often incorporated intraoperative or early postoperative 4 
parathyroid hormone (PTH) measurements, which are not available for preoperative risk assessment. This study 5 
aimed to assess the feasibility and internal validity of a preliminary preoperative risk model for predicting 6 
postoperative hypocalcemia after endoscopic thyroidectomy. 7 
Methods: We conducted a retrospective cohort study of 78 patients who underwent endoscopic thyroidectomy.  8 
Postoperative hypocalcemia was defined as postoperative day-1 serum calcium <2.1 mmol/L and occurred in 10 9 
patients (12.8%). Preoperative serum calcium, PTH, and vitamin D were selected as candidate predictors based on 10 
their clinical relevance and availability before surgery. Because of the limited number of outcome events, the 11 
number of predictors was restricted to reduce model complexity and minimize the risk of overfitting.  Standard 12 
logistic regression, Firth logistic regression, and LASSO regression models were developed. Internal validation was 13 
performed using 1,000 bootstrap resamples to assess optimism-corrected model performance, including 14 
discrimination, calibration, and clinical utility. 15 
Results: The apparent AUC was approximately 0.64 across all three regression approaches. After bootstrap 16 
optimism correction, AUC values decreased to 0.527 (95% CI 0.432 -0.659) for standard logistic regression, 0.529 17 
(95% CI 0.440-0.662) for Firth logistic regression, and 0.537 (95% CI 0.477-0.659) for LASSO regression. Brier 18 
scores ranged from 0.109 to 0.110, and decision-curve analysis showed limited clinical benefit. Among the 19 
evaluated variables, preoperative calcium demonstrated the strongest association with postoperative hypocalcemia.  20 
Conclusion: In this preliminary cohort, a preoperative risk model based on routinely available biochemical variables 21 
showed limited predictive performance after internal validation. These findings suggest that only preoperative 22 
biochemical information may not adequately capture the factors associated with postoperative hypocalcemia after 23 
endoscopic thyroidectomy. Future studies should incorporate surgical and perioperative variables to improve model 24 
performance. 25

Introduction 28 
Endoscopic thyroidectomy has emerged as an alternative to conventional open thyroidectomy, offering comparable 29 
oncologic safety with improved cosmetic outcomes. As with conventional thyroid surgery, postoperative 30 
hypocalcemia remains one of the most common complications following endoscopic thyroidectomy, resulting from 31 
inadvertent injury, devascularization, or removal of the parathyroid glands during dissection. Given the technically 32 
demanding nature of endoscopic approaches and their relatively recent adoption, robust preoperative risk 33 
stratification tools remain limited. 34 
Prior work in conventional thyroidectomy has consistently identified perioperative parathyroid hormone (PTH) 35 
levels as the strongest predictors of postoperative hypocalcemia, outperforming clinical and surgical variables in 36 
both regression-based and machine learning models [1,2]. However, these models have generally relied on 37 
intraoperative or early postoperative PTH measurements, which are not available at the time of preoperative 38 
counseling and surgical planning. A prediction model based on preoperative variables including PTH, calcium, and 39 
vitamin D would allow risk stratification before surgery, potentially informing patient counseling, operative 40 
planning, and the threshold for prophylactic calcium/vitamin D supplementation.  41 
A central methodological challenge in developing such a model is the limited sample size typical of endoscopic 42 
thyroidectomy cohorts, given the procedure's relatively recent introduction and lower case volume compared with 43 
conventional thyroidectomy. In our study, the events-per-variable (EPV) ratio for a three-predictor model is 44 
approximately 3.3, below the commonly cited threshold of 10 EPV historically recommended for stable logistic 45 
regression estimation. Under these conditions, standard maximum-likelihood logistic regression is prone to finite-46

sample bias, particularly with rare outcomes. Firth's penalized-likelihood logistic regression was developed 1 
specifically to address this bias in small-sample and rare-event settings, while least absolute shrinkage and selection 2 
operator (LASSO) regression offers an alternative regularization approach that shrinks coefficients toward zero, 3 
reducing overfitting and improving out-of-sample generalizability at the cost of introducing bias [3,4,5,6]. 4 
Given this, the present study did not aim to establish a definitive, clinically deployable prediction model, but rather 5 
to conduct a preliminary, hypothesis-generating comparison of three logistic regression-based approaches—standard 6 
logistic regression, Firth logistic regression, and LASSO logistic regression —to identify which modeling strategy 7 
offers the most stable and generalizable performance for predicting postoperative hypocalcemia after endoscopic 8 
thyroidectomy using preoperative biochemical variables alone, in preparation for future prospective, adequately 9 
powered validation. 10 
The objectives of this study were: (1) to describe the association between preoperative PTH, calcium, and vitamin D 11 
levels and the occurrence of postoperative hypocalcemia in a retrospective cohort; (2) to develop and internally 12 
validate three candidate prediction models using bootstrap resampling; and (3) to compare the discriminative 13 
performance and stability of the three models and identify the most suitable approach for this small -sample, 14 
preoperative prediction context. 15

Predictor Variables 42

Three preoperative biochemical predictors were selected a priori based on biological plausibility and prior literature 1 
on calcium homeostasis: preoperative intact parathyroid hormone (PTH), preoperative serum calcium, and 2 
preoperative vitamin D. Age, sex, and body mass index (BMI) were additionally recorded as descriptive/optional 3 
covariates but were not included as primary model predictors, given the limited sample size and the a priori focus on 4 
biochemical predictors. There were no missing values for any of the three preoperative predictors or the outcome 5 
variable, allowing a complete-case analysis without imputation. 6 
Statistical Analysis 7 
Data completeness and the pattern of missing data were assessed for all candidate predictors; none were missing. 8 
The distribution of each continuous variable was examined visually and assessed for normality using the Shapiro -9 
Wilk test (preoperative PTH was non-normally distributed and summarized as median [interquartile range]; 10 
preoperative calcium and vitamin D approximated normal distributions and were summarized as mean ± standard 11 
deviation). Patients were stratified into hypocalcemia (n=10) and no hypocalcemia (n=68) groups. Between -group 12 
differences were assessed using the Mann–Whitney U test for continuous variables and Fisher's exact test for sex, to 13 
characterize directional trends rather than to perform formal variable selection, given the pre -specified, biologically-14 
driven choice of predictors. 15 
Three logistic regression-based models—standard logistic regression, Firth logistic regression, and LASSO logistic 16 
regression—were developed, each including all three preoperative predictors. All continuous predictors were 17 
standardized prior to model fitting. Firth's method was implemented directly (rather than via the `firthlogist` 18 
package, which was incompatible with the numpy ≥2.0 environment used), penalizing the log -likelihood with the 19 
Jeffreys invariant prior to remove first-order finite-sample bias. The LASSO penalty parameter was selected via 5-20 
fold cross-validation. 21 
Internal validation for all three models was performed using bootstrap resampling (1,000 resamples with 22 
replacement from the original 78-patient cohort), following the approach described by Harrell and Steyerberg for 23 
optimism correction in small-sample prediction models [7,8]. For each resample, each model was refit and evaluated 24 
both on the bootstrap sample (apparent performance) and on the original cohort (test performance); the average 25 
difference between these estimates across all resamples yielded the optimism estimate, subtracted from the apparent 26 
AUC to derive the optimism-corrected AUC with bootstrap-percentile 95% confidence intervals. As an independent 27 
cross-check, DeLong 95% confidence intervals were also computed for the apparent AUC of each model.  28 
For each model, the following performance metrics were reported: apparent and optimism -corrected AUC with 95% 29 
CI; sensitivity and specificity at the Youden-optimal probability threshold; the Brier score; and calibration-in-the-30 
large (intercept), calibration slope, and the Integrated Calibration Index (via LOWESS), acknowledging that these 31 
apparent calibration estimates are imprecise given the small number of outcome events and were not independently 32 
corrected for optimism. ROC curves, a binned calibration plot, and a decision -curve analysis (quantifying net 33 
clinical benefit across threshold probabilities) were generated for visual and clinical -utility comparison across 34 
models. The three models were compared based on their optimism-corrected AUC and the width of the 35 
corresponding 95% CI as a measure of stability; the preferred model was selected based on (a) highest optimism -36 
corrected AUC, (b) narrowest/most stable CI, and (c) simplicity and methodological appropriateness for small -37 
sample, rare-event data, with the latter prioritized in the event of comparable performance across models. As an 38 
exploratory, non-candidate comparator, a gradient-boosting model was additionally fit to the same data solely to 39 
illustrate the behavior of flexible machine learning algorithms at this sample size.  40 
All statistical analyses were performed using Python (scikit-learn, statsmodels, and dcurves packages), with a 41 
dependency-free implementation of Firth's method and DeLong AUC confidence intervals. All randomness was 42 
seeded (seed = 42) to ensure reproducibility. A two-sided P value <0.05 was considered statistically significant for 43 
group comparisons; given the exploratory nature of this step, no correction for multiple comparisons was applied.  44

Study population 46

A total of 78 consecutive patients who underwent endoscopic thyroidectomy were included. Postoperative 1 
hypocalcemia (day-1 serum calcium <2.1 mmol/L) occurred in 10 patients (12.8%); 68 patients (87.2%) did not 2 
develop hypocalcemia. There were no missing values for any of the three preoperative predictors or the outcome, 3 
and all patients were preoperatively normocalcemic. 4 
Descriptive and group comparison 5 
Table 1 summarizes preoperative characteristics by hypocalcemia status. No individual preoperative variable 6 
differed significantly between groups (all P>0.1), consistent with the limited statistical power afforded by only 10 7 
outcome events; this should not be interpreted as evidence that these predictors are uninformative. Nonetheless, all 8 
three biochemical predictors trended in the biologically expected direction, with lower preoperative PTH, calcium, 9 
and vitamin D in the hypocalcemia group, and preoperative calcium showed the largest relative between -group 10 
difference. Notably, all 10 hypocalcemia cases occurred in women (10/10 vs. 57/68 in the no -hypocalcemia group; 11 
Fisher's exact P=0.341); this numerically striking but statistically non -significant observation is consistent with prior 12 
literature identifying female sex as a mild risk factor for post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia and is reported here as an 13 
exploratory observation rather than a finding. 14

Model coefficients and apparent performance 15 
All three models agreed in the direction of association: lower preoperative calcium, PTH, and vitamin D each 16 
predicted higher hypocalcemia risk, with preoperative calcium the strongest individual predictor in every model 17 
(standardized odds ratio [OR] per 1-SD increase: standard logistic regression, OR 0.649; Firth logistic regression, 18 
OR 0.687; LASSO logistic regression, OR 0.731). Firth logistic regression coefficients sat between the standard 19 
maximum-likelihood estimates and zero, as expected of a bias-reduction method. LASSO logistic regression 20 
retained all three a priori predictors without shrinkage to zero. Apparent (in -sample, uncorrected) AUC was similar 21 
across models—standard logistic regression 0.641, Firth logistic regression 0.644, LASSO logistic regression 22 
0.638—but these estimates are optimistic and were not interpreted as final performance ahead of internal validation.  23 
Internal validation performance 24 
Table 2 summarizes apparent and optimism-corrected performance for each model. After bootstrap optimism 25 
correction (1,000 resamples), the apparent AUC of ≈0.64 for all three models fell to an optimism -corrected AUC of 26 
0.527 (95% CI 0.432–0.659) for standard logistic regression, 0.529 (95% CI 0.440–0.662) for Firth logistic 27 
regression, and 0.537 (95% CI 0.477–0.659) for LASSO logistic regression (Figure 1). The mean optimism was 28 
approximately 0.11 AUC units for all models, consistent with the degree of overfitting predicted at an EPV of 29 
approximately 3.3 [5]. All three 95% confidence intervals crossed 0.5, indicating that none of the models 30 
discriminated meaningfully better than chance after correcting for optimism. DeLong 95% confidence intervals 31 
computed independently on the apparent AUC were wide but consistent in direction and magnitude with the 32 
bootstrap estimates (standard logistic regression 0.460–0.822; Firth logistic regression 0.468–0.820; LASSO logistic 33 
regression 0.459–0.817), providing convergent evidence from two independent methods. 34

Brier scores were low and similar across models (standard logistic regression 0.109; Firth logistic regression 0.110; 1 
LASSO logistic regression 0.109), only marginally below the no-information Brier score corresponding to the 2 
observed event rate (0.128), indicating negligible predictive skill beyond the base rate (Figure 2). Apparent 3

regression 1.405) with small Integrated Calibration Index values (0.015 –0.027); these apparent, in-sample 5 
calibration estimates are imprecise given only 10 events and were not independently corrected for optimism. At the 6 
Youden-optimal threshold, sensitivity was 0.90 for all three models, while specificity was substantially lower 7 
(standard logistic regression 0.38; Firth and LASSO logistic regression 0.44 each), indicating that the models 8 
flagged nearly all true hypocalcemia events at the cost of a high false-positive rate. 9 
Decision-curve analysis 10 
Decision-curve analysis (Figure 3) showed that none of the three models achieved net benefit over a “treat -all” or 11 
“treat-none” strategy across the range of clinically plausible threshold probabilities (approximately 5% –30%), 12 
independently corroborating the absence of clinically usable discrimination suggested by the optimism -corrected 13 
AUC. 14 
Model comparison and selected model 15 
The three models were statistically indistinguishable, with substantially overlapping optimism -corrected AUC 95% 16 
confidence intervals (approximately 0.43–0.66; Table 2). Per the pre-specified selection rule, Firth logistic 17 
regression was selected as the final model: it is the methodologically preferred estimator for small -sample, rare-18 
event logistic regression and demonstrated the narrowest coefficient confidence intervals among the three 19 
candidates, though LASSO logistic regression (which retained all three a priori predictors without shrinkage) 20 
represented an equally defensible alternative. The final Firth model, expressed in original clinical units, was: logit(p) 21 
= 9.475 − 0.0046×PTH(pg/mL) − 4.3098×calcium(mmol/L) − 0.0299×vitamin D(ng/mL). Expressed as odds ratios 22 
per clinically meaningful increment (bootstrap 95% CI), higher preoperative calcium (OR 0.61, 95% CI 0.25 –1.10 23 
per 0.1 mmol/L), vitamin D (OR 0.75, 95% CI 0.32–1.62 per 10 ng/mL), and PTH (OR 0.92, 95% CI 0.39–1.37 per 24 
10 pg/mL) were each associated with lower odds of hypocalcemia, although all confidence intervals crossed 1. For a 25 
hypothetical patient at the mean of all three predictors, the model-predicted risk of hypocalcemia was 13.7%, closely 26 
matching the observed event rate (12.8%). As an exploratory, non-candidate comparator, a gradient-boosting model 27 
fit to the same data achieved an apparent AUC of 1.000, reflecting complete memorization of the training data and 28 
empirically confirming that flexible machine learning approaches are inappropriate at this sample size (EPV ≈3.3) 29 
[5,6]. 30 
Discussion 31 
(Continuous text, no subheadings, per guideline) 32 
This preliminary study compared three logistic regression-based approaches—standard, Firth, and LASSO logistic 33 
regression—for predicting postoperative hypocalcemia after endoscopic thyroidectomy using three preoperative 34 
biochemical predictors (PTH, calcium, vitamin D) in a cohort of 78 patients with 10 hypocalcemia events. All three 35 
models showed similar apparent discrimination (AUC ≈0.64), but this fell to an optimism -corrected AUC of 36 
approximately 0.53 for standard and Firth logistic regression and 0.54 for LASSO logistic regression after bootstrap 37 
internal validation, with all 95% confidence intervals crossing 0.5. Firth logistic regression was selected as the 38 
preferred model based on its methodological suitability for small-sample, rare-event settings and its narrowest 39 
coefficient confidence intervals, though its performance was statistically indistinguishable from the other two 40 
approaches. In every model, preoperative calcium was the strongest individual predictor, and all three predictors 41 
were associated with hypocalcemia risk in the biologically expected direction.  42 
It is important to clarify the basis for selecting Firth logistic regression as the final model, since its optimism -43 
corrected AUC (0.529) was not numerically the highest among the three candidates —LASSO logistic regression 44 
achieved a marginally higher point estimate (0.537) with a narrower confidence interval width (0.181 vs. 0.222 45 
AUC units). However, the 95% confidence intervals of all three models overlapped almost completely 46 
(approximately 0.43–0.66), so this difference cannot be considered a meaningful performance distinction; selecting 47

a model on the basis of a 0.01 difference in AUC under these conditions would itself constitute overfitting to noise. 1 
Model selection therefore followed the pre-specified rule of prioritizing methodological appropriateness when 2 
performance is statistically indistinguishable, rather than chasing the nominally highest point estimate. Firth's 3 
penalized-likelihood approach is the estimator specifically developed to correct the finite-sample bias and separation 4 
problems that arise at low EPV, and is widely regarded in the biostatistics literature as the more principled default 5 
for small-sample, rare-event logistic regression regardless of AUC ranking; LASSO logistic regression, which 6 
retained all three a priori predictors without shrinkage, remains an equally defensible alternative and is reported 7 
alongside Firth logistic regression in Table 2 for transparency. 8 
These findings are consistent with, rather than contrary to, established methodological literature on small -sample 9 
clinical prediction modeling. At an events-per-variable ratio of approximately 3.3, marked optimism (≈0.11 AUC 10 
units) between apparent and internally validated performance was expected [3,4,5,6], and the observed collapse 11 
from an apparent AUC of 0.64 to an optimism-corrected AUC of approximately 0.53 illustrates precisely this 12 
phenomenon. As a further empirical check, an exploratory gradient-boosting model fit to the same data achieved a 13 
perfect apparent AUC of 1.0, reflecting complete memorization rather than genuine discrimination; this reinforces 14 
the appropriateness of restricting model candidates to penalized and bias-corrected regression approaches, rather 15 
than more flexible machine learning algorithms, at this sample size [5,6]. 16 
From a clinical utility standpoint, decision-curve analysis showed no model achieved net benefit over a treat-all or 17 
treat-none strategy across plausible threshold probabilities, and at the Youden -optimal threshold, models achieved 18 
high sensitivity (0.90) only at the cost of poor specificity (0.38–0.44)—a trade-off unlikely to be clinically useful for 19 
guiding individualized postoperative calcium supplementation. One plausible explanation for the limited 20 
discriminative performance is that this cohort was surgically homogeneous, comprising predominantly unilateral 21 
procedures with near-universal unilateral central lymph node dissection; the surgical variables most strongly 22 
associated with hypocalcemia in prior literature (extent of resection, bilateral dissection) therefore varied little in this 23 
sample, leaving preoperative biochemistry alone unable to capture surgical risk that was not well represented in the 24 
cohort. 25 
These results contrast with prior post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia prediction models, several of which reported 26 
considerably higher discrimination (AUC 0.90–0.98) [1,2]; however, those models relied on intraoperative or early 27 
postoperative PTH measurements as the dominant predictor, which by definition capture the degree of parathyroid 28 
injury that has already occurred during surgery. The present model was deliberately restricted to preoperative 29 
variables in order to address an earlier point in the clinical decision-making pathway—namely, preoperative 30 
counseling and surgical planning—and the comparatively limited performance observed here suggests that 31 
preoperative biochemistry alone, without information about the surgical insult itself, may be insufficient to reliably 32 
predict this outcome. 33 
This study has several important limitations. Although the sample size (n=78) exceeded that of our originally 34 
planned preliminary cohort, the number of hypocalcemia events remained small (n=10), yielding an EPV of only 3.3 35 
and correspondingly wide confidence intervals around all performance estimates; this limits statistical power for 36 
detecting true associations and precludes reliable assessment of model calibration beyond descriptive, in -sample 37 
estimates. The retrospective, single-center design and the surgical homogeneity of the cohort (predominantly 38 
unilateral procedures) may limit generalizability to more heterogeneous or higher -risk surgical populations. Only 39 
internal validation was performed; no external validation cohort was available. Given these limitations, and 40 
consistent with the explicitly preliminary framing of this study, the findings should be interpreted as hypothesis -41 
generating rather than confirmatory. 42 
Future work should prioritize prospective, multicenter data collection to achieve an adequate events -per-variable 43 
ratio, incorporate surgical-extent variables (e.g., unilateral versus bilateral resection, extent of lymph node 44 
dissection) alongside preoperative biochemistry, and pursue external validation of the selected model. Despite its 45 
limited discriminative performance, this study demonstrates a methodologically transparent approach to small -46 
sample prediction modeling—reporting an honest, optimism-corrected result rather than an overfit, non-replicable 47 
estimate—which we believe represents the more scientifically defensible posture for a preliminary cohort of this 48 
size. 49 
This finding of limited discrimination should not be equated with a lack of scientific value. A substantial and well -50 
documented problem in the clinical prediction modeling literature is the publication of apparent, in -sample 51 
performance metrics without adequate internal validation, which produces optimistic models that fail to replicate in 52

independent data [5,6]. By explicitly correcting for optimism and reporting the resulting null -ish finding, this study 1 
avoids contributing a non-replicable model to the literature and instead provides an honest evidence base for future 2 
work. The result is also directly actionable: it indicates that preoperative biochemistry alone, in a surgically 3 
homogeneous cohort with limited variation in operative extent, is insufficient to reliably predict postoperative 4 
hypocalcemia, and that future model development should prioritize larger, adequately powered cohorts that capture 5 
both biochemical and surgical predictors. In this sense, a rigorously derived preliminary finding of limited 6 
discrimination constitutes a meaningful and useful contribution, sparing future investigators from over -interpreting 7 
similarly modest apparent performance in comparable small-sample settings. 8
