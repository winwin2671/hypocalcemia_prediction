Introduction 23 
Transoral endoscopic thyroidectomy vestibular approach (TOETVA) has emerged as an alternative to conventional 24 
thyroidectomy, offering comparable oncologic safety with improved cosmetic outcomes. As with conventional 25 
thyroid surgery, postoperative hypocalcemia remains one of the most common complications following TOETVA, 26 
resulting from inadvertent injury, devascularization, or removal of the parathyroid glands during dissection through 27 
the narrow transoral working space. Given the technically demanding nature of the approach and its relatively recent 28 
adoption, robust preoperative risk stratification tools specific to TOETVA are lacking. 29 
Prior work in conventional thyroidectomy has consistently identified perioperative parathyroid hormone (PTH) 30 
levels as the strongest predictors of postoperative hypocalcemia, outperforming clinical and surgical variables in 31 
both regression-based and machine learning models [1,2]. However, these models have generally relied on 32 
intraoperative or early postoperative PTH measurements, which are not available at the time of preoperative 33 
counseling and surgical planning. A prediction model based on preoperative variables including PTH, calcium, and 34 
vitamin D would allow risk stratification before surgery, potentially informing patient counseling, operative 35 
planning, and the threshold for prophylactic calcium/vitamin D supplementation. 36 
A central methodological challenge in developing such a model is the limited sample size inherent to TOETVA 37 
cohorts, given the procedure's relatively recent introduction and lower-case volume compared with conventional 38 
thyroidectomy. In our study, the events-per-variable (EPV) ratio for a three-predictor model is approximately 2.3, 39 
well below the commonly cited threshold of 10 EPV historically recommended for stable logistic regression 40 
estimation. Under these conditions, standard maximum-likelihood logistic regression is prone to complete or quasi-41 
complete separation and finite-sample bias, particularly with rare outcomes. Firth's penalized-likelihood logistic 42 
regression was developed specifically to address this bias in small-sample and rare-event settings, while least 43

Statistical Analysis 39

Data completeness and the pattern of missing data were assessed for all candidate predictors. The distribution of 1 
each continuous variable was examined visually and assessed for normality using the Shapiro-Wilk test. Patients 2 
were stratified into hypocalcemia group (n=7) and no hypocalcemia group (n=35), and continuous variables were 3 
summarized as median (interquartile range) or mean ± standard deviation according to their distribution while 4 
categorical variables were summarized as frequency (percentage). Between-group differences were assessed using 5 
the Mann–Whitney U test for continuous variables and Fisher's exact test for categorical variables.  6 
Three logistic regression-based models including standard logistic regression, Firth logistic regression, LASSO 7 
logistic regression were developed with all three preoperative predictors. All continuous predictors were 8 
standardized prior to model fitting. 9 
Internal validation was performed using bootstrap sample with 1000 resamples from the original 42-patient cohort, 10 
following the approach described by Harrell et al. and Steyerberg et al. for optimism correction in small-sample 11 
models [7,8]. For each resample, the models were refit and evaluated on both the bootstrap sample (apparent 12 
performance) and the original sample (test performance). The average difference between these estimates across all 13 
iterations served as the optimism estimate, which was subtracted from the apparent performance to yield optimism-14 
corrected area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (AUC) values with 95% confidence intervals 15 
(CI). 16 
For each model, the following performance metrics were reported: AUC with 95% CI (primary metric); sensitivity 17 
and specificity at a pre-specified/optimal probability threshold [PENDING: e.g., Youden's index]; and ROC curves 18 
for visual comparison. As exploratory/secondary metrics, calibration plots and the Brier score were additionally 19 
reported where feasible, acknowledging the limited precision of calibration assessment given the small number of 20 
outcome events. The three models were compared based on their bootstrap-derived, optimism-corrected AUC and 21 
the width of the corresponding 95% CI as a measure of estimate stability. The preferred model was selected based 22 
on (a) highest optimism-corrected AUC, (b) narrowest/most stable CI, and (c) simplicity of the final model, with 23 
simplicity prioritized in the event of comparable performance — consistent with methodological guidance favoring 24 
parsimonious models in small-sample prediction research. 25 
All statistical analyses were performed using [PENDING: R version X.X (packages: logistf, glmnet, pROC/rms) 26 
/ Python version X.X]. A two-sided p value <0.05 was considered statistically significant for group comparisons; 27 
given the exploratory nature of this step, no correction for multiple comparisons was applied. 28

This study has several important limitations. The sample size was very small, with only 7 hypocalcemia events, 21 
limiting statistical power and the precision of all reported performance estimates; the wide confidence intervals 22 
anticipated around the AUC estimates should therefore be interpreted with caution. The retrospective, single-center 23 
design may limit generalizability, and only internal validation was performed, without an external validation cohort. 24 
Given these limitations, the title and framing of this work as “preliminary” is intentional, and the findings should be 25 
regarded as hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. Future work should prioritize prospective, multicenter 26 
data collection to achieve an adequate events-per-variable ratio, followed by external validation of the selected 27 
model, and may consider the inclusion of additional preoperative variables to further improve discrimination. 28
